		\item Make the supposed proof given by spaghettifying the circle rigorous using Eudoxus's Method of Exhaustion.  
		Please include in your proof an argument showing that the shape made with the spaghetti from the circle is in fact a triangle.\\

        \solution\\
        \textbf{Claim:} the area of the circle is $1/2 C r$, where $C :=$ circumference of the circle and $r := $ radius of the circle.\\

        \textbf{Proof:} Let $\pi := C/D = C/2r$, where $C :=$ circumference of the circle, $D :=$ diameter of the circle, and $r :=$ the radius of the circle. As shown in the figure in the original homework, start by dividing the circle into $n$ annuli and then ``flatten'' each annuli into a rectangle\footnote{In class, you talked about how this being allowed is questionable, but to me it makes sense because the flattened annuli and the original sliced annuli are topologically identical. I also got a C+ in the intro to topology class so my confidence in anything topology is weak at best}, we will call these flattened annuli ``strips''. Lets proceed by describing the strips.\\

        Each strip will have a unique length depending on where in the circle it originated from. The outermost (now \textit{strippified}) annulus will have length $C$ and the height will be $r/n$, where $n :=$ number of annulus. All annulus will have uniform height $r/n$ by construction. The next annulus will then have length $C - C/n$. The inner most annulus will have length $C - ((n-1)C)/n) = C/n$. We can see the difference in length between any subsequent strips is always $C/n$. Since these strips are decreasing uniformly in length, they can be arranged in a ragged triangle. We can then fit these strips into a triangle with height $r$ and length $C$ which has area $1/2Cr$ as shown in the figure below.

        \begin{center}
            \includegraphics[scale=0.18]{over-estimate.jpg}
        \end{center}
        
        We can see this is an over-estimate of the triangle, and therefore the area represented by the strips is $> 1/2 Cr$. The area described by this arrangement is
        \[a(\text{arrangement}_1) = \sum_{i=1}^n 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n}\]
        We can see $2r \pi (i/n)$ is the length of each strip and $r/n$ is the width. Observe we can then create an under estimate by adjusting the bounds of the summation as shown in the figure below.
        \begin{center}
            \includegraphics[scale=0.15]{under-estimate.jpg}
        \end{center}

        The area of these strips is then
        \[a(\text{arrangement}_2) = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n}\]

        From these summations and the triangle we are approximating, we can then see the following inequality
        \[\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n} < \frac{1}{2} Cr < \sum_{i=1}^{n} 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n}\]

        \begin{center}
            \includegraphics[scale=0.2]{meme.png}
        \end{center}
        
        We can see there is a closed form of the summations above which will make the epsilon argument easier to see.
        \begin{align*}
            \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n} &= 2r\pi \frac{r}{n^2} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} i \\
                                                           &= \frac{2r^2 \pi}{n^2} \frac{n (n-1)}{2}\\
                                                           &= \frac{r^2 \pi (n-1)}{n}
        \end{align*}
        A similar transformation is possible with the other summation
        \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} 2r\pi \frac{i}{n} \frac{r}{n} = \frac{r^2 \pi (n+1)}{n}\]
        Now, observe for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $n \gg 1$ such that
        \begin{align*}
            \left(\frac{r^2 \pi (n-1)}{n}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{2} Cr\right) &< \epsilon\\
            \left(\frac{r^2 \pi (n+1)}{n}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{2} Cr\right) &< \epsilon
        \end{align*}
        Note that by our definition of $\pi$ we can see
        \[C = 2\pi r\]
        Making this substitution we observe
        \begin{align*}
            \left|\frac{r^2 \pi (n+1)}{n} - \pi r^2\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \left|\frac{r^2 \pi n}{n} + \frac{r^2 \pi}{n} - \pi r^2\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \left|\frac{r^2\pi}{n}\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \frac{r^2\pi}{n} &< \epsilon
        \end{align*}
        A similar inequality occurs for our lower bound
        \begin{align*}
            \left|\frac{r^2 \pi (n-1)}{n} - \pi r^2\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \left|\frac{r^2 \pi n}{n} - \frac{r^2 \pi}{n} - \pi r^2\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \left|-\frac{r^2 \pi}{n}\right| &< \epsilon\\
            \frac{r^2 \pi}{n} &< \epsilon\\
        \end{align*}
        We can see from this result, picking $n > \frac{r^2\pi}{\epsilon}$, then $\frac{r^2\pi}{n} < \epsilon$. We are able to obtain a value within $\epsilon$ of $1/2Cr$, meaning the area provided by our triangle approaches the area of the triangle with height $r$ and width $C$. Since the strips partition the circle exactly, the total area of the strips equals $a(\mathbb{C})$ exactly, and we have shown this lies within $\epsilon$ of $\frac{1}{2}Cr$ for any $\epsilon > 0$; Therefore, $a(\mathbb{C}) = 1/2Cr$. $\hfill\blacksquare$

		\item Let $a(\cdot)$ denote the area function.  Let $C_r$ denote a (the) circle with radius $r$. Prove that $a(C_r) = \pi r^2$ using the following:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Define the area of a (the) circle with radius equal to $1$ to be $\pi$.
			\item Euclid's Proposition 2 from Book XII.
		\end{itemize}

        \solution I am going to assume proposition XII.2 is given\footnote{Carter and Derek said this was totally cool to do when I asked them :)}. In a sentence, this proposition states that circles are to one another as the squares on their diameters. Euclid shows this by inscribing polygons which approximate the area of the circle closer and closer as the number of faces on the polygons increase.\\

        Anyway, lets define $a(C_1) = \pi$. By Euclid XII.2, we can see given a circle with arbitrary radius $C_r$
        \[\frac{a(C_r)}{a(C_1)} = \frac{r^2}{1^2} = r^2 \Rightarrow a(C_r) = r^2 \cdot a(C_1) = \pi r^2\]

        Using the result of XII.2, we observe the area of the circle with radius $r$ is $\pi r^2$. $\hfill\blacksquare$
		
		\item \noindent In class on 4/1 I presented the method Newton used to determine the coefficients $a_i$ in what-we-now-call the power series expansion of $(1+x)^{1/2}$. Newton discovered 
		$$(1+x)^{1/2} = 1 + \frac{1}{2}x - \frac{1}{8}x^2 + \frac{1}{16}x^3 -\frac{5}{128}x^4 + \frac{7}{256}x^5 - \cdots.$$
		More generally, using the notation $\alpha^{\underline{k}} = \alpha(\alpha -1)(\alpha -2)\cdots (\alpha - k + 1)$, where $k$ is a nonnegative integer, Newton discovered 
		\begin{eqnarray}(1+x)^{1/2} = 1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{(1/2)(1/2-1)}{2}x^3 + \frac{(1/2)(1/2-1)(1/2-2)}{6}x^3 + \cdots + \frac{(1/2)^{\underline{k}}}{k!}x^k+ \cdots. \label{NewtonSeries}
		\end{eqnarray}

            \solution We\footnote{By \textit{we} I mean my laptop, my coffee mug, the copy of Isaac Asimov's \textit{Prelude to Foundation} that sits on my coffee table, and myself} can heed the suggestion in the prompt and compute the Mclaurin series

                \frac{\pi}{4} &= \int_0^1 (1-x^2)^{1/2}dx = \int_0^1 1 + \int_0^1 \frac{1}{2}(-x^2) + \int_0^1 \frac{(1/2)(1/2-1)}{2} (-x^2)^2 + \int_0^1 \frac{(1/2)(1/2-1)(1/2-2)}{6}(-x^2)^3 + \cdots\\
                              &= 1 - \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{40} - \frac{1}{112} = 0.7994047619
            \end{align*}

            Notice we are substituting $x \to -x^2$ in (1) and then integrating every term. It is cool to see we start with $1$ and then \textit{shave} away pieces until it looks closer and closer to $\frac{\pi}{4}$. If we multiply this result by $4$ we obtain
            \[4 \left(1 - \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{40} - \frac{1}{112}\right) \approx 3.19761\]

            If we were civil engineers, this would be an acceptable approximation! We obtained $\pi$ with 2 digits of accuracy. Define $\Delta \pi_i = |\pi - \pi_{\text{approx.}}|$ to be the error between the actual value of $\pi$ and the approximation obtained at $i$ terms of the Mclaurin series above. For $i = 4$ which we computed above $\Delta \pi_4 \approx 0.056026394$.\\
